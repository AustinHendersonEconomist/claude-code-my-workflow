\begin{frame}{Dementia: The Coming Crisis}
  \begin{columns}
    \column{0.54\textwidth}
      \small
      \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Dementia} (primarily Alzheimer's disease) is the most costly age-related
              condition globally
        \item NZ: $\approx$70,000+ living with dementia (2024); projected 150,000+ by 2050
        \item Global cost: US\$1.3 trillion/year (WHO, 2023) --- largest share of any
              single disease
        \item \textbf{No proven treatment}; heavy burden on \textbf{unpaid caregivers}
      \end{itemize}
    \column{0.43\textwidth}
      \begin{alertblock}{Economic framework}
        \footnotesize Dementia forces us to grapple with:
        \begin{itemize}
          \item The value of cognitive function (QALYs)
          \item Who bears the cost? (patient, family, state)
          \item Long-term vs.\ acute care: different cost structure
          \item Value of end-of-life care and advance directives
        \end{itemize}
      \end{alertblock}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Multi-morbidity: The Dominant Pattern of Older Adult Health}
  \begin{columns}
    \column{0.54\textwidth}
      \small
      \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Multi-morbidity}: two or more chronic conditions simultaneously
        \item Among NZ adults aged 65+: $>$60\% have two or more chronic conditions
        \item The combination is often more complex and costly than the sum of individual
              conditions
        \item Health systems designed for \textbf{single-disease acute episodes} are poorly
              suited to multi-morbidity
      \end{itemize}
    \column{0.43\textwidth}
      \begin{alertblock}{Coordination challenges}
        \small
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Multiple specialists; polypharmacy
          \item Conflicting clinical guidelines
          \item Higher risk of adverse drug interactions
        \end{itemize}
        \footnotesize\textbf{NZ response:} \textit{localities} framework (integrated care)
      \end{alertblock}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Healthy Ageing: The Economics of Prevention}
  \begin{block}{Active Ageing (WHO framework)}
    \small Optimising opportunities for health, participation, and security to enhance quality of
    life as people age --- across the \textbf{whole life course}.
  \end{block}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Economic argument for prevention: investment early in the life course generates
          \textbf{returns} through reduced HC costs later
    \item But: \textbf{discounting} reduces the present value of future cost savings
          (more in Part II, Week 8)
    \item Prevention vs.\ treatment: prevention does not always win on cost-effectiveness grounds
    \item \textbf{NZ Age-Friendly Cities} (WHO-aligned) and the \textit{Healthy Ageing
          Strategy 2016--2026} set the policy framework
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Healthy Ageing: Key Policy Question}
  \begin{alertblock}{Optimal allocation: prevention vs.\ treatment}
    Given resource constraints, what is the optimal split between \textbf{prevention}
    (exercise programmes, falls prevention) and \textbf{treatment} (hip replacements,
    dementia care) for the ageing population?
  \end{alertblock}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The answer depends on discount rates, health production functions, and equity weights
    \item Evidence base for prevention programmes is often weaker than for acute treatments
    \item Political economy: treatment is visible; prevention benefits are diffuse and delayed
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Overlapping Generations Models: A Brief Introduction}
  \small
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{OLG models} (Samuelson, 1958; Diamond, 1965): standard framework for
          analysing ageing in macroeconomics
    \item Basic structure: each generation works when young, retires when old; the young's
          savings fund the old's consumption
    \item Key insight: \textbf{pay-as-you-go} pensions create a transfer from workers to
          retirees each period
    \item Sustainability: wage growth $\times$ workers must exceed pension payout
          per retiree $\times$ retirees
    \item When the dependency ratio rises, adjustment must come via higher contributions,
          lower benefits, later retirement, higher productivity, or immigration (NZ)
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\summaryside{
  \item Dementia will cost NZ (and the world) enormously as populations age, with no proven cure and heavy burden on unpaid caregivers
  \item Multi-morbidity is the dominant pattern in older adults; health systems designed for single acute conditions are ill-suited
  \item Prevention and healthy ageing have strong economic rationale, but discounting reduces their present-value advantage
  \item OLG models: ageing dependency ratios require some combination of higher contributions, lower benefits, later retirement, higher productivity, or immigration
}
