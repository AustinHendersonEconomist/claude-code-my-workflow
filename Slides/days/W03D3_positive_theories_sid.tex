\begin{frame}{The Supplier-Induced Demand Hypothesis}
  \begin{block}{SID hypothesis}
    Due to \textbf{asymmetric information} and the principal-agent relationship, doctors can
    (and do) \textbf{induce demand} for their own services beyond the level the patient would
    choose if fully informed.
  \end{block}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Doctors as agents set quantity, not just prices
    \item In a fee-for-service system, more services $=$ more income
    \item SID predicts: more doctors $\Rightarrow$ more demand for doctor services
          (positive supply--demand correlation)
    \item In a standard market, more suppliers $\Rightarrow$ lower price $\Rightarrow$
          lower quantity (negative) --- the SID paradox
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The Supplier-Induced Demand Hypothesis (cont.)}
  \begin{alertblock}{Target income hypothesis}
    Doctors may try to maintain a ``target income''; if fees are cut, they induce more
    visits to compensate. Both SID and target income predict perverse supply responses.
  \end{alertblock}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item Key implication: cannot use standard competitive supply--demand framework to
          analyse HC markets where providers set quantity, not just price
    \item Empirical challenge: how do we distinguish SID from other explanations?
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Evidence and Controversies}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Small area variation} (Wennberg, 1973): surgical rates vary enormously across
          geographic areas even after controlling for patient characteristics --- suggests
          significant discretion by providers
    \item \textbf{RAND Health Insurance Experiment}: experimental evidence that lower prices
          increase utilisation without clear health gains --- consistent with SID
    \item \textbf{Challenges}: supply--demand correlation could reflect that doctors locate
          where demand is \emph{already} high (endogeneity)
    \item Rice \& Unruh (2009): argue supply and demand are \textbf{not independently
          determined} in HC markets --- undermines standard supply--demand framework
    \item \textbf{NZ context}: SID less of a concern in NZ's largely salaried GP system,
          but DTC pharmaceutical advertising creates industry-to-patient channels for
          inducing demand
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{DTC Pharmaceutical Advertising: A NZ Perspective}
  \begin{columns}
    \column{0.55\textwidth}
      \begin{itemize}
        \item NZ and the US are the \textbf{only two countries} permitting
              direct-to-consumer (DTC) pharmaceutical advertising
        \item DTC lets pharma companies market prescription medicines directly to
              patients via TV, print, and online
        \item Creates \textbf{patient-driven demand} for specific branded medicines ---
              does it improve adherence, or induce unnecessary demand?
      \end{itemize}
    \column{0.42\textwidth}
      \begin{alertblock}{Positive vs.\ normative}
        \small
        \textit{Positive}: DTC $\Rightarrow$ higher branded drug demand and spending.

        \smallskip
        \textit{Normative}: should NZ allow DTC given PHARMAC's budget constraints?
        A live policy debate.
      \end{alertblock}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Reconciling the Evidence: A Framework}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The debate over SID reflects a fundamental challenge in health economics:
          \textbf{supply and demand are not independent} in HC markets
    \item The key insight: doctors shape patient \emph{beliefs} about their need for care ---
          not just the price or quantity
    \item Not all provider influence is malign --- much is genuine expert advice in the
          patient's interest
    \item The policy challenge: maintain \textbf{professional clinical discretion} (which
          benefits patients) while limiting opportunistic SID (which wastes resources)
    \item \textbf{NZ approach}: capitation funding (GP), quality incentive programmes,
          and audit/peer review mechanisms
    \item Week 6 (Hospital Economics) revisits provider incentives at the hospital level
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\summaryside{
  \item The SID hypothesis: providers exploit information asymmetry to induce demand beyond patients' ``true'' preferences
  \item Evidence: small area variation and perverse supply--demand correlations are consistent with SID but not definitive
  \item Target income hypothesis: perverse response to fee reductions
  \item NZ is one of only two countries (with the US) allowing DTC pharmaceutical advertising --- a live policy issue
}
