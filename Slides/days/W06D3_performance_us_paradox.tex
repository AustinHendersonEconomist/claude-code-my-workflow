\begin{frame}{Measuring Health System Performance}
  \begin{columns}
    \column{0.50\textwidth}
      \small\textbf{Five performance dimensions:}
      \begin{enumerate}\small
        \item \textbf{Health outcomes}: mortality, morbidity, life expectancy, HALE
        \item \textbf{Equity}: outcomes/access by income and ethnicity
        \item \textbf{Responsiveness}: patient-centred care, timely access
        \item \textbf{Efficiency}: health outcomes per dollar spent
        \item \textbf{Financial protection}: protection from catastrophic costs
      \end{enumerate}
    \column{0.47\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Commonwealth Fund rankings}
        \small Annual rankings of 11 high-income systems. NZ consistently top 4 overall --- weaker on equity and health outcomes.
      \end{block}
      \begin{alertblock}{The US paradox}
        \small Last despite spending $\approx$17\% of GDP (vs.\ NZ $\approx$10\%).
      \end{alertblock}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{NZ's Performance: Strengths and Weaknesses}
  \begin{columns}
    \column{0.5\textwidth}
      \small\textbf{NZ strengths (Commonwealth Fund):}
      \begin{itemize}\small
        \item Strong primary care system
        \item High patient-reported care coordination
        \item Good preventive care quality
        \item Universal access without financial catastrophe
        \item PHARMAC as cost-effective pharmaceutical funder
      \end{itemize}
    \column{0.47\textwidth}
      \begin{alertblock}{NZ weaknesses:}
        \small
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Long waiting times for elective care
          \item Equity gaps: M\=aori and Pacific face worse outcomes across most indicators
          \item Rural/urban access disparities
          \item Workforce shortages (GP, nursing, specialist)
          \item Mental health access gap
        \end{itemize}
      \end{alertblock}
  \end{columns}
  \small\textit{These weaknesses will be explored in depth in Week 7.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The US Healthcare Paradox}
  \begin{columns}
    \column{0.52\textwidth}
      \begin{block}{Why is US spending so high?}
        \footnotesize
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Administrative complexity (900+ private insurers)
          \item No monopsony bargaining power for prices
          \item Fee-for-service $\Rightarrow$ volume incentives
          \item High specialist and pharmaceutical prices
          \item ``Medical arms race'' in hospital competition
        \end{itemize}
      \end{block}
    \column{0.45\textwidth}
      \begin{alertblock}{Why are outcomes worse?}
        \footnotesize
        \begin{itemize}
          \item 25--30 million uninsured
          \item Geographic and socioeconomic access gaps
          \item Racial and ethnic health disparities
          \item Low primary care relative to specialist
          \item Obesity, diabetes, gun violence
        \end{itemize}
      \end{alertblock}
  \end{columns}
  {\footnotesize Anderson et al.\ (2019): ``It's still the prices, stupid.'' The US pays more for identical services and pharmaceuticals than any other country.}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Lessons from the US Paradox for NZ}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item The US case illustrates the costs of \textbf{market fragmentation} in health care:
          administrative complexity alone wastes $\approx$5\% of GDP vs.\ simpler systems
    \item \textbf{PHARMAC's monopsony power} is a key structural reason NZ pays lower pharmaceutical prices
    \item \textbf{Single-payer administration}: Te Whatu Ora reduces administrative fragmentation
          compared to the DHB era
    \item NZ's Achilles heel is \textbf{equity}: despite universal coverage, access and outcomes
          differ substantially by ethnicity and income --- and will be a focus of Week 7
    \item The equity--efficiency trade-off is not solved by choosing a system type;
          it requires deliberate policy choices within the system
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\summaryside{
  \item Health system performance has five dimensions: outcomes, equity, responsiveness, efficiency, financial protection
  \item NZ ranks well globally (top 4 on Commonwealth Fund) but has equity gaps for M\=aori, Pacific, and low-income populations
  \item US spends $\approx$17\% of GDP on healthcare but ranks last among high-income countries --- the ``US paradox'' reflects fragmentation, administrative waste, and access inequalities
  \item NZ's single-payer, monopsony-purchasing (PHARMAC) model avoids many US-style inefficiencies; Week 7 deepens the NZ analysis
}
