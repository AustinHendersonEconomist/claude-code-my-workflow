\begin{frame}{Education vs.\ Health: Similarities}
  \footnotesize
  Health and education are \textbf{NZ's two largest areas of government spending} ($\approx$13\% of GDP combined), exhibiting the same core economic problems that justify government involvement.
  \vspace{0.1em}
  \begin{columns}
    \column{0.52\textwidth}
      \textbf{Similarities}:
      \begin{itemize}
        \item Both are major areas of government expenditure
        \item Both generate substantial positive externalities
        \item Both involve information asymmetries
        \item Both have principal-agent problems (teacher/student; doctor/patient)
        \item Both are partly ``derived demands'' (skills/income; well-being)
      \end{itemize}
    \column{0.45\textwidth}
      \begin{alertblock}{Important differences}
        \footnotesize
        \begin{itemize}
          \item Education is \textbf{consumed voluntarily}; much health care is not
          \item Outcomes are \textbf{long-run and delayed}; HC outcomes often immediate
          \item Education: \textbf{longer production cycles} (years, not days)
          \item Education has \textbf{multiple outputs}: skills, socialisation, signalling
        \end{itemize}
      \end{alertblock}
  \end{columns}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Market Failures in Education}
  \footnotesize
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Positive externalities}: education raises productivity and social outcomes
          for \emph{others} (innovation spillovers, lower crime, civic participation)
          $\Rightarrow$ private demand \emph{below} socially optimal
    \item \textbf{Capital market failure}: students cannot easily borrow against future income
          to fund current costs $\Rightarrow$ under-investment, especially by low-income families
    \item \textbf{Imperfect information}: students cannot evaluate educational quality before
          purchase; employers face information asymmetry about graduate quality
    \item \textbf{Consumption in childhood}: children cannot make optimal investment decisions
          on their own behalf $\Rightarrow$ government provides compulsory schooling
  \end{enumerate}
  \begin{exampleblock}{NZ response}
    \footnotesize Free compulsory schooling (Years 1--10); subsidised secondary (Years 11--13);
    subsidised tertiary (income-contingent Student Loan Scheme for fees and living costs)
  \end{exampleblock}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The NZ Education System: A Brief Overview}
  \footnotesize
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Early childhood}: regulated, partly subsidised through ECE funding
          ($\approx$20 hours/week free from age 3)
    \item \textbf{Compulsory schooling}: Years 1--10 (ages 5--16); curriculum set by
          Ministry of Education; locally governed
    \item \textbf{Secondary}: free public schooling Years 11--13; NCEA qualification framework
    \item \textbf{Tertiary}: mixture of universities (8), polytechnics (ITPs), w\={a}nanga (M\=aori
          tertiary institutions), and private training establishments (PTEs)
    \item Tertiary funding: \textbf{government subsidises} approximately 75--80\% of domestic
          student tuition through the Tertiary Education Commission (TEC); students pay fees
          (currently capped at $\approx$NZ\$6,000--9,000/year)
    \item \textbf{Free first year}: policy introduced in 2018 --- first year of tertiary
          fully funded by government for domestic students
    \item \textbf{Student Loan Scheme}: income-contingent loans for fees AND living costs;
          interest-free for NZ residents
  \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\summaryside{
  \footnotesize
  \item Education shares health's three rationales for government intervention: externalities, capital market failure, information asymmetry
  \item The six special characteristics of health care markets map similarly onto education markets
  \item NZ has a hybrid system: free compulsory schooling, subsidised secondary and tertiary, income-contingent student loans
  \item Human capital and signalling theories (Weeks 10--11) provide competing explanations of why education raises wages
}
